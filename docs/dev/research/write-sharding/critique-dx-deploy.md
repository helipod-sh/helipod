# Adversarial Review — Lens: DX, Deploy-Anywhere & Buildability ("dx-deploy")

Ground-truth notes I verified in the tree before writing: `.shardKey` exists exactly as claimed (`packages/values/src/schema.ts:97`), `WriteRouter.forward` is the real pre-execution chokepoint (`packages/runtime-embedded/src/runtime.ts:60–64, 152–154`), `write-fanout.ts:50` does `Number(delta.commitTs)` and drops `shardId`, and `kernel.ts:172` really does bake `Number(ctx.snapshotTs)` into `_creationTime`. All three designs cite the seams accurately; none of them invented code.

## A shared attack first (applies to all three, none names it)

**The `shard:`/`shardBy` annotation is redundant with the schema and therefore a drift footgun.** All three require the developer to declare the shard twice: once on the table (`.shardKey("channelId")`) and once per mutation (`shard: "channelId"`). The information to infer the mutation's shard already exists in the validated args (`v.id("channels")`) plus the schema's shard key — yet all three make it a manual annotation whose *mismatch* is caught only at write time. A `messages` mutation that declares `shard: "authorId"` typechecks, codegens, and runs in dev; it errors on its first `ctx.db.insert` — or worse, never errors if it happens to only read. Codegen could infer or at least statically cross-check the annotation against the write set's tables in the common case. B gets partial credit (codegen validates the arg *exists*); nobody validates the arg is the *right* one. For a product whose locked convention is "the typed-client DX is load-bearing," shipping a stringly, drift-prone second declaration is a self-inflicted wound all three share.

## Design A — "One Line, Many Hands"

### (a) Strongest attacks

**A1. The sharding guards are structurally invisible in development — the footgun fires only in production.** A's own Tier-0 story is the attack: at `NUM_SHARDS = 1` (§3.3), *every* shard-key value hashes to shard 0, so the §3.4 kernel guard ("this mutation runs on shard 3 but the document belongs to shard 5") and the §2.2 foreign-sharded-read rejection are **vacuously satisfied in `stackbase dev`, in every test, and in every Tier-0 deployment**. An app author writes a mutation that reads `messages` for two different channels, it works flawlessly for months, and the first `--shards 8` deploy throws runtime errors on code paths that have never once errored before. This is the worst possible shape of DX failure — *dev-green, prod-red* — and it directly falsifies the design's headline claim that "same app code runs unchanged at every tier": the code runs, but its *legality* changes by tier. The fix (a `stackbase dev --shards N` simulation mode) is cheap and obvious, and the design doesn't propose it.

**A2. Two allocation regimes = a permanently forked engine contract.** At Tier 0, the in-memory oracle allocates `commitTs`; in fleet-sharded mode, `DocStore.write()` becomes *allocating* (`nextval` inside the commit txn, §1.1). That is not one seam with two implementations — it is two different *contracts* for who owns the timestamp, selected by deployment mode. The transactor's most safety-critical discipline (allocate→apply→publish under the mutex, `single-writer-transactor.ts:182–228`) now has a variant that only executes in multi-node fleet deployments — precisely the configuration the test suite exercises least (per the project's own memory: the Bun/fleet paths are under-tested relative to Node/vitest). The design calls this "the most invasive diff... in the whole design" in an honorable mention; under a buildability lens it's the top-three risk, not a footnote.

**A3. Min-frontier jitter is an operator-experience tax with a diffuse debugging story.** One shard's slow commit transaction (bounded only by a 5 s `statement_timeout`) or one flapping lease drags F, and the *symptom* is fleet-wide: every subscription's re-runs stall, every scalar RYOW wait lengthens, every replica watermark freezes — including for tenants on perfectly healthy shards. The operator of a self-hosted 3-node fleet sees "the whole app went stale for four seconds" and must know to go find the min-pinning shard. The dashboard shard view is deferred to slice 3, i.e., the observability for the design's own signature failure mode ships last.

### (b) Honesty of stated weaknesses

Mostly honest — the one-Postgres ceiling, min-frontier hostage-taking, and un-OCC'd frontier reads are all named plainly, and weakness 3's "a linguistic fence, not a mechanical one" is admirably blunt. **Understated:** the dev/prod guard vacuity (A1) is not mentioned anywhere, and the dual-allocation-regime fork is demoted to an honorable mention. The "removes the write ceiling" framing is caveated honestly in §10.1.

### (c) Steelman

A is the only design with **zero migration and zero wire change**: `_creationTime` untouched, `StateVersion` untouched, client SDK untouched, scalar RYOW untouched, and slice 1 is gated by *the existing fleet tests passing unmodified* — the cheapest, safest first slice of the three, and it hardens the shipped fleet (kills the skipped-ts-99 class, closes the promotion-fencing hole) even if sharding stops there. A1 is fixable with a one-flag dev-simulation mode; A2 is confined behind the DocStore seam and testable with the PGlite conformance rig the project already owns. If you believe small agents build this incrementally, A's ladder is the one they can actually climb.

## Design B — Per-Shard Logs, Frontier Vectors

### (a) Strongest attacks

**B1. The consistency contract is a four-tier model you must now *teach*, and its anomalies are semantic, not errors.** "Serializable per shard, consistent-cut across shards, causal per session, eventual across sessions" (§4.6) is the contract of a distributed database, not of a Convex-class BaaS whose pitch is "you never think about consistency." Worse than the write-skew everyone shares: B is the *only* design where a subscription can show a cross-group **effect before its cause** (client A mutates group X after seeing group Y; client B's node has X ahead of Y). A and C structurally cannot exhibit this — their frontier is a prefix of one real history. B's mitigation is per-*session* monotonic floors, which means the anomaly is real for every other observer and — the DX killer — **only exists at P>1**. Like A's guard gap, but worse: A's tier gap produces *errors* in prod; B's produces *silently reordered realities* that no test at P=1 can catch and no error message will ever name. This is buried as point 6 of §4 and one clause of weakness 1.

**B2. Slice 0 is a physically-stored index-key migration imposed on every existing app before any sharding value ships — in a product whose Postgres adapter's marquee feature is "no migration step is ever needed."** `_creationTime` is baked into every index key and every pagination cursor (`index-manager.ts:23–32`); B's own weakness 2 admits the migration "touches physically-stored index keys or requires a compatibility epoch in the key codec." An operator with a live self-hosted deployment must eat an index rewrite (or a forever-compat codec) to get to a version whose feature they may never use. And the payoff is *permanently degraded*: cross-group `by_creation_time` becomes NTP-approximate forever, and cross-group pagination becomes a P-way merge with skew fuzz.

**B3. Protocol surface area: B changes the most wires of the three.** `StateVersion.ts` redefined to a node-local sequence (forfeiting the deferred version-gap resync *permanently* — reconnecting clients full-resync forever), RYOW tokens become `(shardId, ts)` pairs, action folding becomes a frontier *map* (`executor.ts:283–292`), `/_fleet/run` returns pairs, sessions carry high-water maps, and the query engine grows a `ClosedFrontier` vector type. Each is individually defensible; together they mean the client SDK, the sync protocol, the executor, and the fleet forwarder all change semantics — the maximum blast radius for the agents building it and for every app embedding the client.

### (b) Honesty of stated weaknesses

**The most honest of the three.** Weakness 1 concedes outright that "the global-line rival design can honestly claim a stronger story here," weakness 2 owns the index-migration bomb, weakness 3 owns the P-frozen bet and the P× operational surface. **Understated:** the cross-session causal inversion (B1) deserved top billing, not fine print; and nowhere does B note that its consistency anomalies are undetectable at P=1, i.e., in every dev environment.

### (c) Steelman

B has the **best Tier-0 code identity** (P=1 is literally today's oracle/ring/lease — no dual allocation regime), the **best blast radius** (a wedged group stalls only itself — the cleanest operator story of the three: "channel X is stale" maps 1:1 to "group 3 is lagging"), and the **cleanest object-storage mapping** (one append stream per group + one conditional-PUT frontier pointer is *exactly* the S3 primitive set; A needs a new sequencer home, C needs manifest choreography). And slice 2's "P=4 inside one process under `stackbase dev`" is the single best buildability idea in any of the three documents — it makes multi-group semantics testable with zero distribution, and if promoted to a permanent dev flag it also closes B's own dev/prod gap better than A or C could. Finally: B's default-deny with `crossShardReads: true` opt-in is the most honest write-skew UX on offer — the anomaly is *named at the call site*, not just in docs.

## Design C — Sequenced Epochs

### (a) Strongest attacks

**C1. Slice 0 is the anti-slice: a bet-the-engine, unflaggable, cross-cutting migration before a single unit of sharding value ships.** Restructuring the bigint (past 2^53 → ts-as-string at every wire edge: `write-fanout.ts:50`, `protocol.ts`, `forwarder.ts:104`), redefining `_creationTime`, re-baselining "131+ tests," and touching transactor + docstore + sync + client + fleet *simultaneously* is precisely the change the project's own build discipline (small agents, independent slices, E2E gates through shipped entrypoints) exists to prevent. The design's own words: "the single most likely place this project eats an outage" and "it cannot hide behind a feature flag." Under a buildability lens that sentence is close to disqualifying — the incremental path is "first, perform open-heart surgery; the incrementality begins afterward." Every app must also take a client-SDK upgrade (protocol v2) before its backend can ship slice 0 — a coordinated upgrade for a self-host product with no fleet-managed clients. Calling that "small, mechanical" is the design's biggest understatement.

**C2. The failure mode is a silent, fleet-wide *world freeze* — the worst operational story of the three.** Because C uniquely serves **all** reads at the closed frontier — even same-shard, even on the writer node (§5) — one wedged-but-lease-holding node freezes *every read in the fleet*, including shards on healthy nodes, for up to lease TTL, while writes keep committing invisibly. A degrades cross-shard surfaces but keeps per-shard hot reads live; B quarantines the sick group. C's operator sees "the entire app is frozen, dashboards included, and nothing is erroring" — the design admits this verbatim and offers a watchdog. For the target persona (solo self-hoster, one VPS, no pager), this is the failure mode you never want to be your flagship.

**C3. Serial-per-shard execution converts a slow mutation from "slow" into "tenant outage," and the intent journal is an unexamined data-governance surface.** One pathological `collect()` head-of-line-blocks its entire shard where today's pipelined OCC would overlap it (admitted). And `intents(path, args, identity, resultValue)` durably journals **every mutation's raw args and identity** — PII, tokens-in-args, GDPR erasure — with "tunable retention" as the entire treatment, plus an admitted 30–60% row-volume tax on the very Postgres whose bandwidth is the named remaining ceiling.

### (b) Honesty of stated weaknesses

Genuinely honest at the top — weakness 3's self-assessment of slice-0 risk is the most candid sentence in all three documents, and weakness 1 doesn't hide the freeze. **Understated:** the forced client-protocol upgrade ("small, mechanical"), the intent journal's privacy/volume surface, and the fact that C is the only design that *by construction* breaks MUST-HOLD 12's letter (Tier-0 byte-identity) — it argues the change is tier-uniform, which is true, but "uniform" here means "every tier, including apps that never shard, observes new ts magnitudes and new `_creationTime` semantics."

### (c) Steelman

C is the only design that solves the two problems A and B both punt to "v1.1's hardest slice": **resharding doesn't exist** (moving a shard = moving a lease, zero data copy, because the substrate is shared and the shard lives in the ts encoding), and **the guards are tier-uniform** — 1024 logical shards exist at Tier 0, so `CrossShardWriteError` fires in `stackbase dev`, on the developer's laptop, on day one. That is the *correct* answer to the dev/prod gap that is my strongest attack on A and B. Add exactly-once client retries across failover (a real, marketable DX feature no ancestor shipped), the un-blocked version-gap resync, and the cleanest "epoch batch = immutable blob" object-storage story, and C is the design whose *year-three* DX and ops are best — if it survives its own slice 0.

## Ranking (dx-deploy lens)

**1. Design A — 7.5/10.** A wins this lens on buildability and blast-radius-of-change: zero migrations, zero wire changes, zero client upgrades, a slice 1 that is a pure hardening win gated by existing tests, and the app-author's world literally unchanged. Its real sins — the dev-invisible guards (A1) and the dual allocation regime (A2) — are the cheapest to fix of any design's top attacks (a `--shards N` dev-simulation flag; conformance-suite coverage of the allocating-store contract), whereas B's and C's top attacks are structural. Min-frontier jitter is a genuine operational tax, but it degrades staleness, not correctness, and it is the price of keeping every scalar surface — which is most of what "Convex-grade DX" means at the protocol level. A survives because its costs are latency and vigilance; the others' costs are semantics and surgery.

**2. Design B — 6/10.** B is the best-engineered *distributed system* of the three — cleanest Tier-0 identity, best failure isolation, best object-storage mapping, most honest self-critique, and the P=4-in-one-process gate is the smartest buildability move in any document. But under a DX lens it sells the product's soul: a four-tier consistency contract, cross-session causal inversion that no dev environment can surface, permanent forfeiture of the scalar version line, and an index-key migration for every existing app as the price of admission. B is what you build when you've accepted you're a database vendor; Stackbase's pitch is that its users never have to think like database customers.

**3. Design C — 5.5/10.** C has the best *ideas* under this lens — tier-uniform guard semantics, no-resharding-ever, exactly-once retries — and the most honest risk register. It loses on the two things my lens weighs heaviest: it cannot be built incrementally (slice 0 is an unflaggable engine-wide migration plus a coordinated client upgrade — hostile to the small-agent, slice-gated process this repo demonstrably runs on), and its signature failure mode (silent fleet-wide read freeze from one wedged node, amplified by all-reads-at-frontier) is the worst possible story for a self-host-anywhere product. If the team had a year and one staff engineer, C might be the right end-state; as the next slice on the shipped fleet, it front-loads the outage and back-loads the value. Notably, C's tier-uniform 1024-virtual-shard guard model and B's multi-group-in-one-process dev mode are both portable into A — the strongest overall move available is A with those two ideas stolen.
